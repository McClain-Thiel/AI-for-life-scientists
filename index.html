Every one of these headlines is technically true and almost completely uninformative. "AI-powered" tells you nothing about what algorithm is running, what data it was trained on, or whether the AI is actually doing anything proprietary. It's become a marketing adjective. Our job today is to get under it.

Clearing the Fog

AI is the big ring — the word that goes in the pitch deck. A marketing umbrella. Machine Learning is the actual thing — a class of algorithms that learn patterns from data. Deep Learning is a subset of ML that uses neural networks. LLMs — ChatGPT, Claude — are a small subset of deep learning. Most of the time when someone says "AI" they're gesturing vaguely at this whole picture.

What can ML actually do?

SUPERVISED

Labeled examples

Learn a mapping from input to known output.

tumor image  →  malignant / benign
UNSUPERVISED

Structure without labels

Find patterns, clusters, or low-dimensional structure.

patient records  →  patient clusters
SEMI-SUPERVISED

A bit of both

Label some examples, infer the rest.

useful when labeling is expensive
Three flavors. Supervised is what you'll see in most commercial AI — examples of inputs and right answers; the model learns the mapping. Unsupervised is useful for exploration — finding patterns you didn't know to look for. Semi-supervised bridges the two. We'll spend most of our time on supervised because it's where the majority of valuable AI products live. Reinforcement learning exists too — AlphaGo, robotics — but it's less common in biotech applications and out of scope today.

The regression demo isn't just about math — it's about the business of AI. When a startup says "our model is 99% accurate on our test set," first question: what did you train on, and is your test set from the same distribution as the real world? Overfitting to a curated dataset is one of the most common ways AI products fail in deployment.

None of these are fundamentally different algorithms. They're all supervised learning — input, output, train on examples. AlphaFold is arguably the most impressive scientific AI of the decade. What DeepMind did was realize that protein structure prediction could be framed as: given this sequence, predict these coordinates. Then they got the data (PDB) and the compute. The algorithm is almost secondary.

How an LLM actually works

"the quick brown fox _____"
P(next word | "the quick brown fox")
jumps 72%
leaps 16%
runs 6%
hops 4%
is 2%

input: all previous tokens   →   output: probability over next token

An LLM does exactly this, repeated thousands of times per response. It takes all the text so far and produces a probability distribution over every possible next word. It samples from that distribution. Then it does it again with the new token appended. That's the whole mechanism. The "chatbot" behaviour is just a thin system prompt on top of this.
[Training Mode] This is what training looks like. The model sees text with the last word removed, predicts, we check, we nudge parameters. Billions of times, hundreds of billions of words → GPT. [Inference Mode] This is what happens when you use ChatGPT. System prompt is invisible to you but the model sees it. You type. The model just keeps completing the document. It doesn't "know" it's a chatbot. [Hide system prompt] Now it's just text completion. That's all it ever was.

AlphaFold is the clearest proof that the input→output framing is not trivial. Biologists spent 50 years on the protein folding problem. DeepMind solved it — not with a revolutionary new algorithm — but by framing it as supervised learning, gathering every known structure in the PDB, and training a very large model. The output is just coordinates. The insight was realizing you could frame it this way. Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 2024 (Hassabis, Jumper, Baker). Three ingredients

ALGORITHM commoditizing — Llama, Mistral, open source
COMPUTE commoditizing — AWS, GCP, Azure
DATA the only durable moat

The algorithm is public. The compute is rented. The data is yours.

Five years ago, a novel algorithm was a real moat. Today Meta releases Llama, Mistral releases Mixtral, Google releases Gemma — all open source, all state-of-the-art. Compute is a commodity you rent by the hour. What you cannot rent, cannot download, cannot replicate overnight — is proprietary, labeled, domain-specific data. That's Tempus. That's why they're worth $10B. Not the algorithm. Ten years of matched clinical + genomic records.

Gerald shipped the model. A month later his supplier changes. Fish look a bit smaller and more orange under the new lighting. To a human they're obviously the same kind of thing — to the model they're a new distribution. Accuracy drops from 97% to 68%. This is distribution shift.

In vs. out of distribution

DERMATOLOGY AI trained on light skin  →  −15% accuracy on dark skin

SEPSIS MODEL AUC 0.83 at Hospital A  →  0.63 at Hospital B

RARE DISEASE 10k common cases in training  →  near-random on 200 rare cases

Three clinical examples. Dermatology models fail on dark skin because training sets were overwhelmingly light-skinned. Sepsis models lose 20 points of AUC moving between hospitals. Rare diseases are out-of-distribution by definition. The dangerous part is the model's confidence doesn't drop — it still outputs "97% malignant." You only find out in deployment.

Where AI wins. Where it doesn't.

GOOD AT
  • Consistent, repeatable patterns
  • Large labeled datasets
  • Clear input / output framing
  • Measurable, objective outcomes
  • High-volume decisions
pathology imaging · drug toxicity · protein structure
STRUGGLES WITH
  • Distribution shift
  • Tiny datasets
  • Novel biology with no prior data
  • Causal reasoning
  • High-stakes, irreversible decisions
rare diseases · first-in-class mechanisms · individual patient outcomes
Useful heuristic. AI is uncannily good at tasks with clean labels and massive training sets. It falls over on tasks that require reasoning about things it hasn't seen. Biotech has lots of both kinds of problems — and the valuable thing as an investor is being able to tell which side a given problem lives on.

Who actually makes money?

WRAPPER "AI-powered X" ★
DOMAIN BUILDER Tempus · Recursion ★★★★
FOUNDATION OpenAI · Anthropic ★★★
COMPUTE AWS · GCP · Azure ★★★★
HARDWARE NVIDIA ★★★★★
The AI value chain. Left to right: hardware (near-monopoly), compute (oligopoly), foundation models (funded), domain builders (real moats possible), wrappers (commodity). Value accretes where scarcity is. NVIDIA's moat is hardware — nobody else makes H100s. Domain builders' moat is data. Algorithms commoditise. Wrappers are SaaS with AI marketing. Callback to the outer ring of the AI circle — "marketing umbrella"? That's mostly wrappers.

Before you evaluate anything else, figure out where they sit. Foundation is mostly out of scope for early stage. Wrappers are fine businesses but they're SaaS — no data moat, the model doesn't get better as they grow, anyone can replicate them in a weekend. The middle is where you want to spend your time.

What makes a real data moat

EXCLUSIVE ACCESS

Clinical partnerships, proprietary instruments. Data you only see if you own the pipeline.

COST TO GENERATE

Wet-lab experiments, rare patient cohorts. Data that takes real money and real time.

NETWORK FLYWHEEL

More users → more labels → better model → more users. Usage itself generates training data.

REGULATORY / PERMISSIONED

Can't scrape. Can't replicate. Access governed by contracts or regulation.

"Data is a moat" is lazy shorthand. Actual moat comes from one of these four mechanisms. Ask which one they're claiming. If the answer doesn't map to any of these, their "data moat" is fake — just a dataset someone else can replicate in a weekend.

1. Are you building a model, or calling an API?

2. What does your training data look like?

These two questions short-circuit hours of confused conversation. "Building a model" means training infrastructure, labeled data, evaluation framework, ML engineer on the team. "Calling an API" means they have a product, maybe a good one, but no AI moat. Neither is bad — but they're priced differently and they scale differently.

Red flags

What they sayWhat it means
"We use AI / we're AI-powered"Almost certainly a wrapper.
"We fine-tuned GPT on our data"Fine-tuning ≠ moat unless the data is irreplaceable.
"Our AI learns over time"From what? What are the labels? What's the loop?
"We'll use public / scraped data"Anyone can do this. No moat.
"Can't state input and output clearly"They don't have a real ML problem yet.
"99% accuracy on our test set"What test set? Same distribution as production?
"Works great — in our demo environment"Distribution shift in waiting. Ask Gerald.
"No ML engineer on the team"If the algorithm is the product, who's building it?
The "99% accuracy" line deserves time — test-set leakage and distribution shift are the two most common failure modes. A model trained on clean lab data that fails in messy clinical deployment is one of the most common biotech AI disasters. The "works great in our demo" row is the direct callback to Gerald's fish.

Green flags

What they sayWhat it means
"Can state input and output precisely"They have a model, not a vibe.
"We have exclusive access to X data"Real moat — especially clinical, lab, genomic.
"More users → more labels → better model"Network effect built into the ML itself.
"We built the dataset ourselves"Hard to replicate — wet lab or clinical partnerships.
"We beat [baseline] by X% on [benchmark]"Scientific rigor. Reproducible.
"We know where the model fails"They've actually evaluated it.
"Domain expert + ML cofounder"Label quality requires domain knowledge.
"We built the dataset" is the green flag I weight most heavily in biotech. Wet-lab experiments to generate training data are expensive, slow, hard to replicate. A company systematically generating labeled biological data for two years has a structural moat. The algorithm almost doesn't matter.

Whole talk in one slide. AI is pattern matching. The pattern is a mapping from input to output. The quality of that mapping depends almost entirely on the quantity and quality of labeled training data. Ask these three questions in order. If answers are concrete, specific, and honest about limitations — that's a team that knows what they're building. If the answers are vague, or they jump straight to the demo — be skeptical.
