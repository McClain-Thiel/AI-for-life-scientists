The basics of ML

Chapter card. We spend the next block building one mental model: every ML system is input → model → output, learned from examples. We'll see it in linear regression, protein structure (AlphaFold), and language (LLMs) — same pattern, different framing.

Clearing the Fog

Machine learning: algorithms that learn patterns from data
instead of being explicitly programmed.

AI is the big ring — the word that goes in the pitch deck. A marketing umbrella. Machine Learning is the actual thing — a class of algorithms that learn patterns from data. Deep Learning is a subset of ML that uses neural networks. LLMs — ChatGPT, Claude — are a small subset of deep learning. Most of the time when someone says "AI" they're gesturing vaguely at this whole picture.

What can ML actually do?

SUPERVISED

Labeled examples

Learn a mapping from input to known output.

tumor image  →  malignant / benign
UNSUPERVISED

Structure without labels

Find patterns, clusters, or low-dimensional structure.

patient records  →  patient clusters
SEMI-SUPERVISED

A bit of both

Label some examples, infer the rest.

useful when labeling is expensive
Three flavors. Supervised is what you'll see in most commercial AI — examples of inputs and right answers; the model learns the mapping. Unsupervised is useful for exploration — finding patterns you didn't know to look for. Semi-supervised bridges the two. We'll spend most of our time on supervised because it's where the majority of valuable AI products live. Reinforcement learning exists too — AlphaGo, robotics — but it's less common in biotech applications and out of scope today.

The architecture is just the function that does the mapping. Classification and regression are the two main output types — predicting a category vs. predicting a number. Hold this mental model. None of these are fundamentally different algorithms. They're all supervised learning — input, output, train on examples. AlphaFold is arguably the most impressive scientific AI of the decade. What DeepMind did was realize that protein structure prediction could be framed as: given this sequence, predict these coordinates. Then they got the data (PDB) and the compute. The algorithm is almost secondary.

AlphaFold is the clearest proof that the input→output framing is not trivial. Biologists spent 50 years on the protein folding problem. DeepMind solved it — not with a revolutionary new algorithm — but by framing it as supervised learning, gathering every known structure in the PDB, and training a very large model. The output is just coordinates. The insight was realizing you could frame it this way. Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 2024 (Hassabis, Jumper, Baker). How an LLM actually works

"the quick brown fox _____"
P(next word | "the quick brown fox")
jumps 72%
leaps 16%
runs 6%
hops 4%
is 2%

input: all previous tokens   →   output: probability over next token

An LLM does exactly this, repeated thousands of times per response. It takes all the text so far and produces a probability distribution over every possible next word. It samples from that distribution. Then it does it again with the new token appended. That's the whole mechanism. The "chatbot" behaviour is just a thin system prompt on top of this.
[Training Mode] This is what training looks like. The model sees text with the last word removed, predicts, we check, we nudge parameters. Billions of times, hundreds of billions of words → GPT. [Inference Mode] This is what happens when you use ChatGPT. System prompt is invisible to you but the model sees it. You type. The model just keeps completing the document. It doesn't "know" it's a chatbot. [Hide system prompt] Now it's just text completion. That's all it ever was.

What ML can('t) do

Chapter card. Now that we know the mechanism, we ask when it works. The answer turns on one assumption (IID) and one reality (data quality). Understand those two and you understand most of why AI products succeed or fail in deployment.

Three ingredients

ALGORITHM commoditizing — Llama, Mistral, open source
COMPUTE commoditizing — AWS, GCP, Azure
DATA the only durable moat
Five years ago, a novel algorithm was a real moat. Today Meta releases Llama, Mistral releases Mixtral, Google releases Gemma — all open source, all state-of-the-art. Compute is a commodity you rent by the hour. What you cannot rent, cannot download, cannot replicate overnight — is proprietary, labeled, domain-specific data. That's Tempus. That's why they're worth $10B. Not the algorithm. Ten years of matched clinical + genomic records.

When ML works

ML is a pattern-recognition algorithm.

  1. 1 You can define what "good" means. Labels. An evaluation metric. Ground truth you can measure.
  2. 2 You have a lot of data. Thousands to millions of labeled examples of the pattern.
  3. 3 Your goal is to automate a difficult task. Something a human can do — but slowly, expensively, or inconsistently.
Three preconditions. If all three hold, ML is likely to work well and deliver real value. If any one is missing — no clear metric, no data, or the task doesn't need automation — the ML project is probably a waste of money.

When doesn't ML work?

WHAT WE ASSUME

▬ training data - - held-out test

Trial patients  ≈  test patients
test accuracy looks great

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS

▬ training data ▬ real-world patients

Trial patients  ≠  real-world patients
accuracy drops in deployment

Clinical trial example — the audience lives with this exact problem. Trial patients are screened: ages 35–65, no comorbidities, specific demographics. The held-out test set comes from the same trial → same distribution → great accuracy. Then the drug ships. Real patients include 80-year-olds, diabetics, patients on 12 other medications. That's a different distribution. The model has never seen it. Technical note: this is the IID assumption. Training and test are assumed to be drawn from the same distribution P. When that breaks, all bets are off.
Phase 1 (training): slide N from 0 upward. With 1 sample the histogram is a single spike. With 10 it's noisy. With 100 it converges to the true N(0,1) PDF (yellow dashed). The green curve is the Gaussian fit — the "model". As N grows the fit locks onto truth. Phase 2 (OOD): click "show out-of-distribution samples." Red dots appear near x=2.8 — drawn from a different distribution. The model was never shown anything in that region. Its predictions there are nonsense — but the model itself is not aware of that.

In vs. out of distribution

DERMATOLOGY AI trained on light skin  →  −15% accuracy on dark skin

SEPSIS MODEL AUC 0.83 at Hospital A  →  0.63 at Hospital B

RARE DISEASE 10k common cases in training  →  near-random on 200 rare cases

Three clinical examples. Dermatology models fail on dark skin because training sets were overwhelmingly light-skinned. Sepsis models lose 20 points of AUC moving between hospitals. Rare diseases are out-of-distribution by definition. The dangerous part is the model's confidence doesn't drop — it still outputs "97% malignant." You only find out in deployment.

Where AI wins. Where it doesn't.

GOOD AT
  • Consistent, repeatable patterns
  • Large labeled datasets
  • Clear input / output framing
  • Measurable, objective outcomes
  • High-volume decisions
pathology imaging · drug toxicity · protein structure
STRUGGLES WITH
  • Distribution shift
  • Tiny datasets
  • Novel biology with no prior data
  • Causal reasoning
  • High-stakes, irreversible decisions
rare diseases · first-in-class mechanisms · individual patient outcomes
Useful heuristic. AI is uncannily good at tasks with clean labels and massive training sets. It falls over on tasks that require reasoning about things it hasn't seen. Biotech has lots of both kinds of problems — and the valuable thing as an investor is being able to tell which side a given problem lives on.

The value chain

Where the money goes, where the moats live, and how to tell them apart.

Chapter card. Mechanism, then mechanics, now the business. Five layers from NVIDIA hardware to application-layer startups — and four types of real data moat. This is the lens you'll use when you read an AI biotech deck next week.

Who actually makes money?

APPLICATION the product users touch
"AI-powered X" startups · internal pharma tools
DATA SUPPLIER proprietary, labeled data
Tempus · Flatiron · 23andMe · clinical trial sponsors
FOUNDATION general-purpose model
Llama · Mistral · Gemma (+ OpenAI, Anthropic — closed)
COMPUTE GPUs by the hour
AWS · GCP · Azure · Anyscale
HARDWARE near-monopoly
NVIDIA
BIG PHARMA
eats the top layers
Roche · Pfizer · Novartis · Recursion · Insitro
Five layers, bottom up. Hardware — NVIDIA, a near-monopoly. Compute — rented by anyone with a credit card. Foundation — a commoditising layer; state-of-the-art open weights are 6–12 months behind the frontier. Data supplier — proprietary data at scale is hard to replicate. Application — the surface users touch. The key insight for a biotech audience: Big Pharma doesn't sit at one layer. They own clinical data (data supplier), they fine-tune or train their own models (foundation), and they build internal apps (application). Rented compute, owned stack above it. That's the moat structure you're competing against. So when a pitch says "we're an AI-first biotech", the question is: which layer? And does that layer survive pharma doing it themselves?

Which layer are they in?

FOUNDATION

Train a general model from scratch

Llama · Mistral · OpenAI · Anthropic

Internet-scale pre-training. Capex at nation-state scale.

$10B+ to compete.
DATA SUPPLIER

Own the data, fine-tune on top

Tempus · Flatiron · 23andMe · most pharma

Proprietary, labeled domain data. The thesis turns on the data, not the algorithm.

Defensible if the data is real and exclusive.
APPLICATION

Product on someone else's model

"AI-powered X" · most startups

Prompt engineering + API. No training, no proprietary data.

SaaS economics, not AI economics.
Three archetypes. Foundation is mostly out of scope for early-stage biotech — too expensive, wrong timezone. Application-layer companies are fine businesses but they're SaaS with AI branding — no data moat, anyone can clone them in a weekend. The middle — data supplier — is where biotech investments can actually have a moat. Pharma sits across all three (see previous slide), so ask: how does this company's position survive pharma doing the same thing in-house?

What makes a real data moat

EXCLUSIVE ACCESS

Clinical partnerships, proprietary instruments. Data you only see if you own the pipeline.

COST TO GENERATE

Wet-lab experiments, rare patient cohorts. Data that takes real money and real time.

NETWORK FLYWHEEL

More users → more labels → better model → more users. Usage itself generates training data.

REGULATORY / PERMISSIONED

Can't scrape. Can't replicate. Access governed by contracts or regulation.

"Data is a moat" is lazy shorthand. Actual moat comes from one of these four mechanisms. Ask which one they're claiming. If the answer doesn't map to any of these, their "data moat" is fake — just a dataset someone else can replicate in a weekend.

1. Are you building a model, or calling an API?

2. What does your training data look like?

3. How will you know it's still working in deployment?

These three questions short-circuit hours of confused conversation. (1) "Building a model" means training infrastructure, labeled data, evaluation framework, ML engineer on the team. "Calling an API" means they have a product, maybe a good one, but no AI moat. (2) Training data tells you whether the moat is real or rented. (3) Distribution shift is how deployed ML fails — if they can't tell you how they monitor it, they don't know whether their model is still right.

The mental model

x: difficulty  ·  y: defensibility  ·  bubble size: reward

Same canvas, three dimensions. X is how hard to build and validate. Y is how defensible once built. Bubble size is the prize. Top-right is the frontier: clinical data platforms (Tempus, Flatiron) — very defensible because the data takes a decade to accumulate. De novo generative biology is the biggest bubble — largest potential prize — but it's on the right edge because the wet-lab validation loop is slow and capital-intensive. That slow loop *is* the moat. Top-left is the rare sweet spot: pathology imaging AI. Reasonably well-framed problem, real regulated buyer, medium defensibility because your label pipeline and clinical partnerships are hard (not impossible) to replicate. Middle-right: rare-disease diagnostics. High value, hard, OOD by definition — tempting but fragile. Bottom row is where most pitches live and where most pitches die. AlphaFold-derived tools are easy to build but undefendable (the model is free). "AI-powered X" is the smallest bubble — no data, no model, commoditises in a weekend.
